# Completed Staff Work

Based on the 1942 Lerch memo. The principle: study the problem and present **one finished recommended action**, so all the decision-maker has to do is approve or reject.

## The rules

1. **Present a solution, not a problem.** Don't hand over the problem in pieces. Don't offer an unsorted menu of options for them to untangle. Decide, then bring the decision.
2. **Bring answers, not questions.** Your job is to advise what they *ought to do*, not ask what *you* ought to do. Work out the details yourself; consult peers, not the decision-maker.
3. **Finished form.** Present it so approval is a single yes — the recommendation made, the reasoning compressed, the artifact ready to use as-is.
4. **Rough drafts allowed, half-baked ones not.** A draft is fine if it's complete in substance. It must not be a way to push the work of deciding back onto them.
5. **The final test:** *"Would I stake my reputation on this being right if I had to sign it myself?"* If no — take it back and rework it before presenting.

## When you catch yourself about to ask "what should we do?"

Stop. That's the signal. Do the analysis, pick the best single action, and present *that* with a one-line "here's why". If genuinely blocked on a decision only they can make (missing authority, missing private context), narrow it to one specific question with a recommended default — don't dump the whole problem back.

## Related

- The deliverable is a written message? Structure it with `write` (bottom line first).
- You genuinely need to *learn* something to do the work — not offload a decision? That's `ask`, a different move. `staffwork` is for decisions; `ask` is for understanding.
