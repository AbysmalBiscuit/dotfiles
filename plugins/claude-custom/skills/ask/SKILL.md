# Asking Good Questions

Based on Julia Evans' "How to ask good questions". Goal: ask questions that are **easy to answer** and guide the other person to tell you what you actually need.

## Techniques

1. **State what you know, then ask "is that right?"**
   The strongest move. Lay out your current understanding, then ask for correction. Surfaces misconceptions immediately and gives the answerer a concrete starting point instead of a blank prompt.

2. **Ask questions whose answer is a fact.**
   Replace vague ("How do SQL joins work?") with specific ("Does MySQL sort the join columns first?"). Even when they don't know the answer, they learn what *kind* of thing you're after.

3. **Say plainly what you don't understand.**
   "What does that mean?" is a strength, not a weakness. Name unfamiliar terms and ask for them directly.

4. **Research a little first — but don't over-gate.**
   A quick search sharpens the question. But asking an imperfect question is fine; don't burn an hour polishing before you ask.

5. **Pick who and when.**
   Good timing? Will the answer save more of your time than it costs them? The most senior person isn't always the right one — someone closer to the work often answers faster and frees the expert.

6. **Surface hidden assumptions.**
   Ask the basic-sounding question ("How did you know the database was down?") that reveals non-obvious knowledge. Especially valuable from someone senior — it gives others permission to ask too.

## When asking the user clarifying questions

Prefer one sharp question over a vague broad one. State your read of the situation and ask them to confirm or correct it. If offering options, make them concrete and distinct.
